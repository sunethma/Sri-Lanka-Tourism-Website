Must Visit National Parks in Sri Lanka

Yala National Park

the south eastern region of Sri Lanka and extends over two provinces of Hambantota district of southern province and Monaragala district of Uva province.At Palatupana, the well-designed visitor center provides the information to the tourists and assign a tracker to all incoming vehicles.Dawn and dusk bring about the best timing for Yala Safari tours in the Yala National Park.The gateway to Yala National Park is Tissamaharama.


Wild Life

Of all the National Parks in Sri Lanka, Yala National Park gives the best opportunity to witness Sri Lanka's broad variety of wildlife: colorful painted stork in troops are seen perched at the shores of lagoon where the crocodiles too have chosen to doze off; lovely fantailed peacocks in their resplendent blues and greens parade about amidst the woods where monkeys hang, leap and chatter; in the bush jungle are the Elephants; crossing the tracks and wandering off into the thorny scrub jungle is the star attraction of the park: the leopard.

Udawalawe National Park

Dry Monsoon forest with an annual rainfall of 1520mm has mean day & night temperature are 29 & 24 degrees centigrade respectively. Udawalawe National Park is located 180 km from Colombo in south-central Sri Lanka. The park is situated just south of the Central Highlands, of which escarpment brings about an enrapturing backdrop. At the center of the park lies a reservoir which provides water for agriculture and hydro-power generation.


Wild Life

Udawalawe is one of the best places in Sri Lanka to see wild Asian Elephants throughout the year. There are about 500 elephants in the park and they often roam in herds of up to 100. Endemic birds include the Sri Lankan junglefowl, Sri Lanka spurfowl and Sri Lanka green pigeon. The Udawalawe Elephant Transfer Home is an Elephant orphanage located within the Udawalawe National Park. Among the other bird species seen are Spot-billed pelican, Little cormorant, Grey heron, Indian pond heron, Cattle egret, Great egret, Little egret, Intermediate egret, Painted stork, Woolly-necked stork, Yellow-wattled lapwing, Green bee-eater, Crested treeswift. During the migrant season of birds (Nov to March): Booted eagle, Common kestrel, Harris's hawk, Rosy starling, Black-capped kingfisher, Wood sandpiper, Common sandpiper, Little ringed plover, Whiskered tern, Western yellow wagtail, Forest wagtail & Citrine wagtail.

Minneriya National park

The area is situated in a dry zone of Sri Lanka and receives an average rainfall of 1,500–2,000 millimetres (59–79 in). The lowest temperature and highest of the park are 20.6 °C and 94.1 °F respectively. The main habitats of Minneriya are of several types, including low-canopy montane forests, scrublands, grasslands, rocky outcrops, and wetlands. Main sources of water for the tank are a diversion of Amban River and Elahera canal.


Wild Life

Other wildlife includes Purple faced langur monkeys, wild boar, sambar and spotted deer, buffalo and rarely sighted leopards and sloth bears. Among the bird populations are Sri Lanka Trogon, racquet tailed drongo, endemic yellow fronted barbet, endemic Sri Lanka spurfowl, white necked stork and lesser adjutant stork. In the rest of the year the elephants tend to migrate to nearby Minneriya and Kaudulla national Parks.

Wilpattu National Park

Wilpattu National Park, the largest wildlife sanctuary in Sri Lanka spans an area of no less than 131,693 hectares with altitude ranging between the sea-level and 152 meters. The best time to visit the park is during the months of February and October. A unique complex of over 50 wetlands called 'Villu' is the most prominent topographical feature of the national park. Annual temperature in the Park is 27.2 Celsius and its annual rainfall is approximately 1000 mm.


Wild Life

Wilpattu National Park's natural habitats include coastal belt, natural lakes (villus), rocky outcrops, scrublands and dense forest. Butterflies recorded include the Great Eggfly, Blue Mormon, Common Mormon and Common Rose Great Orange Tip. In the large Villus are Pond turtle (Melanonchelys trijuga) and the Soft shelled turtle (Lissemys punctata). Apart from those two mammals are Asian Elephants (Elephas maximus), Spotted Deer, Barking Deer, jackals, sambhur, barking deer, mouse deer Wild Pig, Water Buffalo (Bubalus bubalis) and Mugger Crocodiles.

Horton Plains National Park

At an altitude of 2,100 meters above sea level, Horton Plains spreads across over 3,169 hectares of the highest tableland of Sri Lanka. The plains and adjoining Peak Wilderness constitute Sri Lanka's most important catchment area of almost all major rivers. Horton Plains was declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site on 30th July 2010. The climate of Horton Plains is that of a wet Montane forest with an average annual temperature of 14-16 Centigrade. Ecological importance of Horton Plains

On a clear morning the World's End affords the view running to the southern coast of Sri Lanka. The panoramic and distant views are bound to get obscured by the mist from around 10am onwards. Especially in the rainy months of May to July, the mist is particularly thick.


Wild Life

Horton Plains is home to Sambar Deer, Bear Monkey and Toque Monkey, Fishing cat, Otter and all of the Montane endemics of Sri Lanka. Other highlights are the Himalayan migrants Pied Thrush, Kashmir Flycatcher Indian Pitta and Mountain Hawk Eagle. Horton Plains is the only national park in Sri Lanka where elephants aren't seen at all. Until a century ago, Horton Plains was rich with Elephants but they were hunted down to extinction by British colonialists. Sinharaja Forest Reserve

UNESCO World Heritage Site Sinharaja Rain Forest is located in Sabaragamuwa and Southern provinces of the south-west lowland wet zone of Sri Lanka. Most of the area of Sinharaha forest was originally declared a forest reserve under the Waste Lands Ordinance in 1875. In 1978, Sinharaja Rain Forest was included in the international network of biosphere reserves as part of UNESCO's Man and the Biosphere (MAB) program. In 1988, it became Sri Lanka's first natural site to be added to the UNESCO World Heritage list. Annual rainfall ranges from 3614mm to 5006mm and temperatures from 19°C to 34°C.


Wild Life

Sinharaja Rain Forest is home to numerous indigenous birds such asthe Ceylon Hanging Parrot (Loriculus beryllinus), the Ceylon Grey Hornbill (Ocyceros gingalensis), the Ashy-headed Laughing Thrush (Garrulax cinereifrons), Layard's parakeet (Psittacula calthripae), the Jungle fowl (Gallus lafayetii), the Spur fowl (Galloperdix bicalcarata), the Ceylon Wood Pigeon (Columba torringtonii), the Brown- capped Babbler (Pellorneum fuscocapillum), the Red-faced Malkoha (Phaenicophaeus pyrrhocephalus) and the Ceylon Blue Magpie (Urocissa ornate) among others, are prominent in this area. 19 of Sri Lanka's 20 species of endemic bird species in Sri Lanka are found in the Sinharaja Reserve. Endemism among mammals and butterflies is greater than 50. Reptiles and amphibia include python, green pit viper, hump nosed lizard and rough-nose horned lizard (Ceratophora aspera).

Bundala National Park

Bundala National Park is located 251 km southeast of Colombo, close to the City of Hambantota in the Southern Province of Sri Lanka. While the average Temperature is 27 degrees Celsius, the annual rainfall varies between 900mm to 1300mm. Dry season' falls between May and September. Best time to visit is September to March: during this period migratory birds arrive at the park. Climate at the Bundala National Park


Wild Life

Each and every species of waterbird found in Sri Lanka is believed to visit Bundala National Park. Among them are the Broad-billed Sandpiper, Curlew and Greenshank Golden and Kentish Plover, Large and Lesser Sandplovers. The coastal area attracts Loggerhead Turtle, Green Turtle, Hawk's bill turtle, and Olive Ridley: four out of Sri Lanka's five marine turtle species. Among them are civets, wild boars, Grey & Ruddy mongoose, porcupine, giant Indian palm squirrels, black-naped hares, Spotted Deer, Wild buffalo, the endemic, jackal and fishing & Rusty Spotted cats. The lagoons at the park attract a great variety of aquatic birds: among them are ibis, pelicans, painted storks, Black-necked Stork, terns, gulls, sand-pipers, snipes, teals, egrets, and spoonbills.

Gal Oya National Park

The Gal Oya National Park is located 376 km northeast from Colombo. Inginiyagala affords the tourists the opportunity of a boat trip around the great Senanayake Samudra reservoir. The Jeep safaris that consist of two tracks of 5km and 13 km are ideal to catch the sights of elephants and leopards. Boat safaris land in an island within the reservoir called "Bird Island" swarming with birds. Together with Senanayake Samudra Sanctuary (9,324ha), Gal Oya Valley North-East Sanctuary (12,432ha), Gal Oya Valley South-West Sanctuary (15,281ha), the whole Gal Oya Reservation makes an area of 62, 937 ha of wildlife conservation.


Wild Life

Gal Oya National Park is a sanctuary to 32 species of terrestrial mammals. The highest populations are common Langur (Presbytis Entellus) and Toque Macaque (Macaca Sinica). About 150 species of birds of 334 species of Sri Lanka are seen at the park. The Indian Cormorant, Oriental Darter, Grey Heron and Lesser Whistling Duck are some of the common water birds of the Senanayake reservoir.
